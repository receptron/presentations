# Beyond the Sea of App Icons

Maria makes soap. The lavender batch is her first new product in a year — she cured it for four weeks, photographed it yesterday against the good linen backdrop, and this morning she wants to tell her customers it exists.

Here is what that takes. She opens her notes app and drafts a caption. She opens the photo editor and crops the picture, then stops — last month's posts used a particular warm filter, and brand consistency matters even for a one-woman company — so she opens Instagram, scrolls her own feed to figure out which filter it was, returns to the editor, and applies it. Then Instagram proper: paste the caption, upload the image, add the hashtags. Then Facebook: paste again, discover the line breaks vanished, restore them by hand. Then X: the caption is forty characters too long, so she writes a shorter one on the spot. Somewhere in the middle she answers a customer's message asking whether the lavender bar uses essential oil or fragrance, which means one more app and one more context.

Twenty minutes. And every individual step was easy — that is what makes it maddening. Nothing crashed. No screen was confusing. Each program did exactly what its designers intended. The twenty minutes disappeared into the seams *between* the programs: remembering which one does what, ferrying the same text and image across six of them, honoring each one's small dialect of buttons and limits. Maria's actual expertise — soap, and knowing her customers — was needed for about ninety seconds of it.

What she wanted was one sentence: *"Announce the lavender soap, purple theme, everywhere we post."* There is no icon on her home screen that accepts that sentence.

## Apps solved capability, never intent

Look at any phone: a glittering sea of icons, each square promising some power. The metaphor made sense when it was invented. In the 1980s, applications were islands of function in an unexplored ocean, and putting a picture on each island was a genuine act of mercy — it replaced memorized incantations with things you could see and click. But forty years on, each island has evolved its own customs, gestures, and grammar, and we have trained ourselves to live like digital migrants, switching dialects a dozen times an hour to talk to our own machines.

The deeper problem is structural, not cosmetic. An app is a bundle: an interface, some logic, and a slice of your data, sealed together by a vendor and shipped as a unit. Capability lives *inside* the bundle; intent almost always crosses bundle boundaries. "Announce the soap" touches a text editor, an image editor, and three publishing platforms. "Plan the trip" touches search, maps, notes, and a calendar. So every task begins not with the task but with a choice — *which app?* — followed by navigation, followed by manual copying across the seams. The app model gave us fifty years of accumulating capability and left the integration of that capability, the part that actually corresponds to what anyone wants, as an unpaid job for the user.

## The feature ratchet

There is an economic engine under the bundle problem, and it explains why every app you own keeps growing. A software product raises its price by doing more: each new tier needs a feature to justify it, so capabilities accrete, release after release, whether or not they belong.

Watch one capability — graphing — make its rounds. Your spreadsheet ships a charting engine, naturally. But so does your project tracker, because dashboards justified an upgrade. So does your accounting package, your note-taking app, your CRM (it calls them analytics), your survey tool, your fitness app. Each implemented charting separately, charges for it separately, and grew more complex and more expensive in the process. You own half a dozen charting engines, pay for all of them, and have learned each one's dialect of the same idea. The bloat is rational — for the vendor. A sealed bundle has no other way to grow its price than to swallow more features. For the user, it means every app slowly becomes a worse version of every other app.

Now run the inversion. If a conversation sits in front of all your applications, a capability like graphing can live *there* — once. No Mac application ships its own scroll bar; the operating system provides it, and every application is simpler because of that. In the same way, when the AI layer owns graphing, your ledger never ships chart code. Ask to see quarterly revenue and the AI reads the numbers from the ledger and draws them with the one graphing capability everything shares — a capability that improves once and improves everywhere. Each application shrinks toward what is irreducibly its own: the shape of its data, its rules, a plain view of its records, and, when a bespoke surface is genuinely needed, an interface the AI generates on request. Simpler to build, simpler to trust, and — since features no longer have to justify pricing tiers — cheaper. Part 4 of this series follows that shrinking all the way down.

## Fifty years of moving the translation

The history of interfaces is one long negotiation over who does the translating. The command line made humans learn the machine's grammar — `cd`, `ls`, flags and pipes — powerful, and priced accordingly in fluency. The graphical interface moved much of that burden to the machine: icons and menus meant you could recognize instead of recall. It made computing visible, and therefore usable, and it is the paradigm we still live in.

Then, in late 2022, the translation flipped direction. The measurable thing that changed with ChatGPT was not answer quality; it was the unit of interaction. People stopped issuing queries — "best italian restaurant near me" — and started holding conversations: "Where should I take my wife for dinner? She doesn't eat shellfish." The computer began speaking *our* language, ambiguity and all, and hundreds of millions of people adopted the habit faster than any interface shift I have witnessed in my career.

But chat, for all that, is a scroll. It is linear, like a text adventure: superb for questions, awkward for making things. A designer needs a canvas, an analyst needs a live table, and no amount of eloquence in a transcript substitutes for either. I will spend Part 2 of this series on that problem, because it has a specific fix and the industry has begun converging on it. For now the point is narrower: chat proved that understanding is possible, and simultaneously proved that understanding alone is not an interface for work.

## The two pillars

I now think the AI-native future of computing rests on two pillars, and that the interesting thing is their fusion.

The first is **the computer that understands**. Natural language becomes the primary interface; you express intent and the machine, not you, figures out which capabilities that intent requires. This is the pillar the last three years have mostly been about, and its ceiling is well documented. A computer that understands but cannot build is a concierge standing in front of fixed apps — Siri's limit for a decade. It can open the restaurant app for you; it cannot make you the thing the app doesn't offer.

The second is **the computer that builds**. Given the intent, the AI generates the artifact — the document, the table, the form, eventually the interface and the logic behind it — fitted to this user, in the same conversation that expressed the need. This pillar already works, but so far mainly for one profession: anyone who has watched Claude Code turn a paragraph of English into a working program has seen it. Its ceiling, alone, is that it serves people who can read what it wrote. Generation without understanding is a power tool for programmers.

I spent 2025 convinced the first pillar was the whole revolution — that conversation replacing navigation was the story. Building prototypes changed my mind. The moment a computer both understands the intent and builds the response, something categorical happens to the app. Maria's sentence gets accepted: the caption is drafted in her voice, the image is edited toward the brand palette she once described, the three platform variants are composed to each platform's limits, and she reviews one screen instead of operating six. The "app" that did this did not exist before her sentence and has no reason to persist after it.

## One conversation, start to finish

Here is the pattern at full length, in a setting with more at stake than soap.

You are preparing for a board meeting, and you say to your computer: *"I need to explain our Q4 results to the board."* A table appears — Revenue, Expenses, Profit, empty. *"Revenue was $2.1M, expenses $1.6M."* The table fills; a chart materializes beside it. *"Compare that to Q3."* Last quarter's figures appear and the chart becomes a trend. *"Now draft the summary document."* An outline opens, sections already pulling from the table. *"Add context about why expenses grew."* You explain out loud — the seasonal hires, the freight costs — and your explanation lands as structured prose. Then: *"Actually, turn this into a short video I can send to the directors who can't attend."* The document becomes a narrated script; images generate for each section; a few minutes later you are watching a three-minute presentation.

You never opened a spreadsheet application, a word processor, or a video editor. Notice which pillar did what. Understanding chose the tools and carried context across every step — "compare that," "add context," "turn this" all resolved against a shared conversation, the kind of references that die at every app boundary today. Building authored the artifacts: the table's columns, the document's outline, the script's narration were generated for this meeting, not selected from anyone's template gallery. Neither pillar alone produces this. Together they change what software *is*.

**The app dies twice: you stop choosing apps, and apps stop needing to pre-exist.**

## Others have noticed

I am not the only one who thinks the app is in trouble; people with considerably more at stake have said so, in more and less careful ways, and the discourse is worth mapping precisely.

In December 2024, on the BG2 podcast, Satya Nadella said of business applications: "that's probably where they'll all collapse... in the agent era... they are essentially CRUD databases with a bunch of business logic. The business logic is all going to these agents." Note the "probably" — he hedged, and the "SaaS is dead" headlines that followed were the media's sentence, not his. But a CEO whose company sells more business applications than nearly anyone describing them as databases whose logic migrates to the AI tier is a remarkable data point about the first death of the app.

Andrej Karpathy has been circling the second. His talk at Y Combinator's AI Startup School in June 2025, "Software Is Changing (Again)," argued that Software 1.0 was code written by humans, 2.0 was neural-network weights, and 3.0 is prompts in plain English — "your prompts are now programs." At Tesla he watched the neural network "quite literally eat through the software stack," and he expects the third generation to do the same; his earlier LLM-as-operating-system framing — the model as CPU, the context window as RAM — is the lineage this whole line of thinking descends from, mine included. That sounds like Nadella's side of the argument. It is not. In the same talk, Karpathy defended the application layer: "you don't want to just directly go to ChatGPT," he said; his picture of the near future is partial-autonomy apps with an "autonomy slider," designed so a human can verify the AI's work at a glance — "less Iron Man robots and more Iron Man suits" — and when people declared 2025 the year of agents, he pushed back: "this is the decade of agents." I read his caution not as a rebuttal but as a design requirement, one this series takes up directly in Part 3.

Two smaller signals. In November 2025, Anthropic and OpenAI — direct competitors — cosigned MCP Apps (SEP-1865), a standard for tools returning interactive interfaces into a chat window; when rivals standardize the substrate, the substrate is no longer speculative, and the substrate they chose is conversation. And SaaS-Bench, a May 2026 benchmark, found that even the strongest models completed fewer than 4% of 106 long-horizon tasks across 23 real SaaS systems — with the scope stated carefully: that result measures *GUI-driving* agents, end-to-end, clicking through interfaces built for human hands and eyes. I read it as evidence about architecture rather than ability: making an AI impersonate a human in front of a human interface is the wrong plan, which is an argument for intent flowing to tools and APIs directly — the shape described above — not against agents.

Here is the map, then. Nadella predicts the logic migrates but names no architecture. Karpathy supplies the operating-system frame but defends a distinct app layer where humans verify machines. The generative-interface efforts, MCP Apps included, let AI deliver or select the interface — over schemas and business logic that a vendor still authors and fixes in advance. Even the closest published cousin to where this series is headed — the "agent-native architecture" essay from Builder.io (May 2026), which gives humans and AI agents equal access to the same application actions — keeps every one of those actions developer-authored: equal *access*, not authorship. Nobody in this discourse, as far as I can find, claims the full fusion: the AI authoring all three layers — interface, schema, logic — per user, per need. That unclaimed square is where this series lives.

## The part I cannot wave away

Two objections to this vision are serious enough that they deserve better than a paragraph of concession, so let me state them at full strength.

The first is discovery. A grid of icons is a bad interface, but it is a *legible* one: every capability you own is sitting there, visibly, and menus double as documentation of what is possible. A blank conversation offers nothing. Amelia Wattenberger put the design version of this sharply — good tools signal what they can and cannot do, and a chat box signals neither. If you do not already know that the computer can turn your quarterly numbers into a narrated video, no affordance will ever tell you; the burden shifts from *finding the feature* to *imagining the possibility*, and imagination is far less evenly distributed than the ability to tap an icon. Maria benefits only if it occurs to Maria to ask. Generated, mixed interfaces — conversation that produces visible, clickable surfaces — recover some of this, but I will not pretend the problem is solved. It may be the hardest open problem in the whole program.

The second is trust, and it compounds when the computer builds. When Maria posted through six apps, she saw every screen; tedium was the price of verification. When one sentence fans out into three platform posts, an edited image, and a customer reply, what exactly went out under her name? An interface that acts on your behalf must earn the right to, and the honest answer is that it earns it the way Karpathy's Iron Man suit does — by making its work fast to inspect and cheap to reject, never by asking to be believed. And beneath trust sits reliability: what stops generated artifacts from being confidently wrong, and who guarantees a generated table computes what it claims? Those questions have real answers involving constrained languages and validation, and they get their own treatment in Part 3; what a "correct" piece of software even means when it is built for one user gets its own in Part 5. Deferral is not evasion — each is a full argument, and this article only claims the two pillars are worth those arguments.

## Where I am testing this

I have not been writing any of this from an armchair. Since 2025 I have been building MulmoClaude, an open-source reference implementation, to test both pillars against real work — starting with whether tools returning live interfaces into a conversation could replace app-switching for tasks like the board-meeting sequence above. Mostly they could, and I wrote at the time as if understanding were the whole revolution. What the prototype actually taught me was the second half: the moments that felt like a different kind of computing were not when the system *picked* the right tool but when it *authored* something that had never existed — a patient-intake form designed mid-conversation, a document composed for one request. Understanding was the door; generation was the room.

The feature ratchet, meanwhile, resolved exactly as the argument predicts. MulmoClaude's accounting application contains no chart code at all. Graphing exists once, as a shared capability the model can invoke for any application; ask about quarterly revenue and the model reads the ledger and draws. The application stays what it should have been all along — a schema, its rules, and a plain view of the books. The findings from all of this, good and bad, supply the concrete material for the rest of the series.

The road map is short. Part 2 shows why conversation needs a canvas and how tools that return interfaces provide one; Part 3 argues that deliberately limited languages are what make AI-built software trustworthy; Part 4 follows the logic to its end, where an application is just a schema, its records, and a model to run them. Parts 5 and 6 turn to consequences — the collapsed economics of software built for one person, and what happens to the boundary between the agent and its workspace when the agent keeps what it builds.
