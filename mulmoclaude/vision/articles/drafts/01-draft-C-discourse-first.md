# Beyond the Sea of App Icons

In December 2024, on the BG2 podcast, Satya Nadella — chief executive of Microsoft, the largest seller of business software on earth — predicted the collapse of his own product category. "The notion that business applications exist, that's probably where they'll all collapse, right, in the agent era," he said. Business applications, in his telling, "are essentially CRUD databases with a bunch of business logic," and "the business logic is all going to these agents." Notice the hedge — "probably" — which most of the headlines dropped. He did not say SaaS is dead. He said something more precise: the logic that makes an application an application is migrating to a new tier, and "once the AI tier becomes the place where all the logic is, then people will start replacing the back ends."

Six months later, Andrej Karpathy — former AI director at Tesla, founding member of OpenAI, and one of the more careful public thinkers about what language models actually are — gave a talk at Y Combinator's AI Startup School called "Software Is Changing (Again)." His framing: Software 1.0 was code written by humans; Software 2.0 was neural network weights; Software 3.0 is prompts in plain English — "your prompts are now programs." He described how, at Tesla, the neural network "quite literally ate through the software stack," and he expects the third generation to do the same: "a huge amount of software to write and rewrite."

That sounds like Nadella's side of the argument. It is not. In the same talk, Karpathy defended the application layer: "you don't want to just directly go to ChatGPT... it makes a lot more sense to have an app dedicated for this." His picture of the near future is partial-autonomy apps — purpose-built interfaces with an "autonomy slider," designed so a human can verify the AI's work at a glance. "Less Iron Man robots," as he put it, "and more Iron Man suits." When people declared 2025 the year of agents, he pushed back: "I get very concerned... this is the decade of agents."

Then there are the people advising the incumbents. Bain & Company's Technology Report 2025 addressed SaaS vendors directly — "Disruption is mandatory. Obsolescence is optional" — and told them where the walls hold: not the interface. "Your data is your moat," plus business logic hardened into the data model itself: approval flows, state transitions, compliance rules built in, so that "any external agent should have to validate through your system of record." Taken seriously — and it deserves to be — this is the claim that in accounting, scheduling, and contracts, the value was never the buttons. It was the invariants: the rules that keep the books balanced no matter who, or what, is typing.

Three positions, then. Nadella: the logic leaves the application. Karpathy: the code gets rewritten, but applications survive as the surface where humans verify machines. Bain: surrender the interface if you must, but the schema and its rules stay home. These are smart people disagreeing in public about what a computer program will even *be* in ten years, and I think all three are describing the same elephant from different sides.

## What the argument already concedes

Start with what nobody in this debate disputes anymore: conversation is becoming the primary interface. The strongest evidence is not a prediction but a signature. In November 2025, Anthropic and OpenAI — direct competitors — cosigned MCP Apps (SEP-1865), a joint standard for delivering interactive interfaces inside a chat conversation, building on Ido Salomon's earlier MCP-UI work. Rival labs do not co-author plumbing for a paradigm they consider optional. When the two largest players agree on the substrate, the substrate question is settled; the open questions live above it.

Second, the tempting middle path — keep today's applications and have AI operate them for you, clicking through the same screens a human would — is measurably failing. SaaS-Bench, a May 2026 preprint from Shi, Li, Ma and colleagues, gave agents 106 realistic long-horizon tasks across 23 real SaaS systems. The strongest model completed fewer than 4% of tasks end-to-end. The result needs honest scoping: it tests GUI-driving agents only, the strictest end-to-end criterion (partial-credit scores run much higher), and it has not been peer-reviewed. But the direction is hard to miss. Software interfaces were designed for human eyes and human patience; making an AI impersonate a human user inherits every accident of that design. If agents are going to do real work, the shape of the software has to change — which is exactly the terrain Nadella, Karpathy, and Bain are fighting over.

## Two capabilities, one unclaimed square

Here is the pattern I see when I lay the positions side by side. The debate keeps circling two distinct capabilities and arguing about each one separately.

The first is **the computer that understands**: natural language becomes the primary interface, and you express intent instead of navigating software. This is Nadella's agent tier, and it is the half of Karpathy's talk where the LLM is the new operating system — the CPU, with the context window as RAM.

The second is **the computer that builds**: the AI generates software — not just text about software — in response to a need. This is the other half of Karpathy's talk, where prompts are programs and English is a programming language. It is also the capability Bain is betting stays fenced: agents may consume the vendor's system, but they don't get to author its schema.

Each capability alone has a ceiling, and we have already run both experiments at planetary scale. Understanding without building is Siri: a concierge stationed in front of fixed applications, able to do anything somebody pre-built and nothing else. Ask it something no product manager anticipated and the understanding is worthless, because there is nothing behind the counter. Building without understanding is code autocomplete, Copilot circa 2022: genuinely powerful, and useful only to programmers, because the request has to arrive already shaped like code.

Map the discourse against those two capabilities and something odd appears. Nadella takes the first seriously and is nearly silent on the second — he says the back ends get replaced, not who or what authors their replacements. Karpathy, to his credit, holds both — but keeps them deliberately apart, with the human-verified app as the joint between them. Bain concedes the first and explicitly fences off the second. Vercel's generative UI, the MCP Apps standard itself — these let a model *choose* or *trigger* interfaces that developers built in advance, which is the first capability wearing the costume of the second.

Nobody in the verified record, as far as I have been able to find, defends the fourth square: the position that the same system understands the request *and* authors the software — the interface, the data schema, and the business logic — fitted to one specific user, in the same conversation that expressed the need. That fusion is the thesis of this series. When the two capabilities operate together, software stops being something you select and starts being something that materializes from intent.

## The view from the user's chair

The debate above is conducted entirely from the supply side — vendors, labs, consultancies. The evidence for the fusion is sitting on the demand side, on every phone's home screen.

Consider Maria, who makes and sells soap. She wants to announce a new lavender bar. Today that means: open a notes app to draft the text; open a photo editor for the image; try to remember which filter keeps her brand consistent; open Instagram, then Facebook, then X; paste the text into each; upload the image to each. Twenty minutes of operating software, roughly zero of which was spent on soap. Every one of those apps is well made. The failure is not in any of them; it is in the seams between them — and the seams are where Maria lives.

This is the sea of app icons, and it is worth remembering how we got here. The command line demanded that humans learn the computer's grammar — `cd`, `ls`, incantations. The graphical interface, the great leap of the 1980s, replaced incantations with pictures; computing became visible and therefore usable. But the GUI shipped with an organizing unit — the application — and forty years on, each app is an island with its own customs and dialects. Apps solved *capability*: there is an app for nearly everything. They never solved *intent*: no app knows what you are actually trying to do, and every task begins with a question the task itself never asked — "which app do I open?"

Then, for the first time in decades, the interface moved. When ChatGPT arrived, the unit of interaction changed from the query to the conversation; people stopped typing "best italian restaurant midtown" and started asking where to take their wife for dinner. That is the first capability — the computer that understands — arriving in public. But chat alone is not the answer either, and honest observers said so early. Amelia Wattenberger's critique holds: a blank text box has no affordances — nothing about it tells you what it can and cannot do — and a linear transcript is a fine shape for questions and a poor one for making things. Designers need canvases; analysts need dashboards. Chat without the second capability is a very articulate concierge in front of the same locked doors.

## One conversation

Here is what the two capabilities look like running together — the interaction the architecture points to. It is a composite of experiments, not a product demo, and Part 2 of this series covers the mechanism that makes it possible.

You have a board meeting. You say: "I need to explain our Q4 results to the board." A table appears — Revenue, Expenses, Profit — with empty cells. Nobody shipped that table as a feature; it was authored in this moment, for this sentence. "Revenue was 2.1 million, expenses 1.6." The cells fill; a chart forms beside them. "Compare that to Q3." Last quarter's figures appear and the chart becomes a trend. "Now draft the summary document." An outline materializes, its numbers pulled from the table you just dictated. "Add context about why expenses rose" — you explain out loud, and your explanation lands as structured prose. Then: "Actually, make this a video I can send to the directors who can't attend." The document becomes a narrated script; images generate for each section; a few minutes later you are watching a three-minute presentation.

At no point did you open a spreadsheet application, a word processor, or a video editor. More telling: several of the artifacts along the way — that particular table, that outline — never existed as products at all. They were built, used, and discarded inside one conversation.

This is why I have come to think of the shift as a double dissolution. The app dies twice: you stop choosing apps, and apps stop needing to pre-exist.

## The case against

The objections to this picture are serious, and the strongest ones come from inside the argument, not outside it.

The first is discovery. The icon grid, whatever its sins, is a catalog: every capability on your phone is advertised by a small picture you can point at. A conversational system that can do nearly anything looks exactly like one that can do nothing — a blank box. If you don't know a travel-guide video is possible, you will never ask for one, and no menu exists to tell you. This is Wattenberger's affordance critique aimed at the whole paradigm rather than just chat, and I don't believe anyone has fully answered it. The honest response is that discovery becomes the system's job rather than the user's — the computer that understands you must also volunteer what it could do for you — and that this is a design obligation the field has barely started paying.

The second is trust. When you operate a spreadsheet yourself, you know what happened, because you did it. When a system assembles the pipeline invisibly, the burden of verification goes up precisely as the burden of operation goes down. This is where Karpathy's Iron Man suit argument lands, and I read it as a requirement rather than a refutation: interfaces in this paradigm exist substantially *so that humans can verify quickly* — the chart is not decoration, it is an audit surface. And beneath trust sits the harder question of whether generated software can be made reliable at all — who checks a schema no engineer reviewed, what happens to Bain's invariants when the user's own workspace has to enforce them. Those questions deserve more than a paragraph, and they get it: the harness that constrains generation is Part 3 of this series, and the fate of the schema is Part 4.

## What I built to find out

I did not arrive at the two-pillar framing by reading the discourse; I arrived at it by building against it and being corrected. In 2025 I built MulmoChat, an open-source prototype ([github.com/receptron/MulmoChat](https://github.com/receptron/MulmoChat)), to test whether one conversation could replace app-switching — tools returning live interfaces into the dialogue instead of text. It could, and I wrote at the time as if understanding was the whole revolution. What the prototype actually taught me was the second half: the moments that felt like a different kind of computing were not when the system *picked* the right tool, but when it *authored* something that had never existed — a patient-intake form designed mid-conversation, a document composed to fit one request. Understanding was the door; generation was the room. The follow-on work in 2026 has been about making that second capability trustworthy, and the findings — including the ones that disappointed me — fill the rest of this series.

Part 2 takes up the mechanism: how a conversation can hold real interfaces without retreating to windows and icons, and why two rival labs just standardized half of that idea. Parts 3 through 6 follow the second pillar all the way down — the harness that makes generated software safe, the architecture in which the application itself becomes data, the economics of software built for exactly one person, and what happens when the agent keeps what it builds.
