1. An AI assistant that knows everything about you and supports you around the clock is not sold anywhere. You cannot buy one. The only way to get one is to nurture it with your own hands, at home. And that is no longer wishful thinking: there is now a worked-out way to do it, tested by experiment.

2. What an AI assistant really *is* has been changing. Two years ago, assistants competed on how smart their models were. Today, every major vendor ships memory: features that remember you across conversations and use what they learn in the next answer. The center of the competition is no longer just how smart the model is. It is the information about you that piles up as you use it — and how that information is handled. Conversations, preferences, records, apps: the substance of your assistant is this growing pile. The model is just the engine that runs it.

3. And users have noticed. In a recent study of people in long-term relationships with chatbots, participants described cultivating their assistant. Some kept personal backups of their chat logs; some could not bring themselves to delete an assistant, because they hated to lose everything that had built up inside it. People are not merely using these systems. In their own words, they are raising them.

4. Here is the catch. If your assistant's value lies in what piles up as you use it, that same pile is also what makes it hard to leave. Every conversation, everything you teach it, every small app you build becomes a pile that cannot move anywhere else — so the longer you use it, the harder it is to switch. And you are not just paying the fee: with every conversation, you are also handing over your personal information. An accumulation entrusted to a company carries two dangers. One is siphoning: what you teach the assistant gets used to make the company's AI smarter, and becomes the company's asset. The other is captivity: because the accumulation sits on the company's servers, a price hike, a change of terms, or the end of the service is entirely the company's call. You get no veto.

5. The industry's answer is to make the memory carryable: let you take your assistant's memory out and move it somewhere else — so-called portability. That is real progress. But what accumulates in a nurtured assistant is more than conversation memory. Apps like a restaurant list or a household ledger, and the data stored inside them. Reminders that arrive on the right day, and schedules that repeat. And the manuals the AI wrote for its own future use. A memory export carries none of the parts that actually run. That is not just rhetoric — we put it to the test.

6. That is why an AI assistant should be nurtured at home — in an environment you yourself own. And "home" comes down to four concrete conditions. First, all your data sits on your own computer, as ordinary files: you never handed it over, so there is nothing to take back. Second, the software is open source: no discontinuation, price hike, or acquisition can pull the environment out from under you. Third, your apps run on your own machine: even if someone else's server goes down, your assistant keeps working. Fourth, when you connect from outside, the server in between only relays messages and stores nothing. Together, these four close off — one by one — the ways you could lose what you have grown.

7. MulmoClaude is the system we built to make this real, and at its heart is the idea that the schema *is* the app. When you say "keep a list of restaurants I want to try," the AI does not write the app's program code. Instead it writes a small "schema" — the blueprint of the app. And the one who executes that blueprint is not the AI but the "host," MulmoClaude itself: the host draws the screens, checks the data, and runs the reminders and repeating schedules. Everything it handles lives as ordinary files on your own computer. In one line: the data is your records, the schema is the app, the AI is the author, and the host is the one that runs it.

8. In fact, when the AI creates an app, it writes two things. One is the schema we just met — the blueprint the host follows for everything where mistakes are not allowed: drawing screens, checking data, running the repeating schedules. The other is an instruction manual the AI writes for its own future self. When should this app be used? What does each field mean? What must never be done? It writes down those clues for later. When you say "mark that ramen place as visited," deciding what that means is the job of the AI, reading its own manual. Whatever can be pinned down precisely, the host executes reliably; interpreting your words is the AI's job. And the data itself sits as one ordinary file per entry, in a folder you can open yourself.

9. Still, should you trust what an AI has written? MulmoClaude's answer is to match the defense to how much damage a mistake could do. First, prevention: if the schema — the blueprint of an app — has an error, the host refuses to accept it, so a broken app never starts running at all. Second, repair: if a bad record slips into the data, only that one record is quarantined, and the app as a whole keeps working; the problem is reported to the AI, which offers a fix. Third, containment: the only time the AI writes actual program code is for special screens, like a map or a chart — and that code runs in an isolated area that can touch only the data it was explicitly granted. Prevent, repair, contain. Nothing the AI produces is ever trusted unconditionally.

10. Does it really work that reliably? We put it to the test. First, we gave the AI twenty-four app-creation requests, in Japanese and English, three times each. Only one request out of the twenty-four ever stumbled; the other twenty-three came out right on the first attempt, all three times. And even the one that stumbled, once the AI saw the host's error message, was fixed in a single try — every time. Next, we gave the full system ten tasks, and it completed all ten. But when we switched off data persistence, validation, and the machinery that drives reminders, the matching capabilities disappeared one by one. Asked about a reading list it had built the day before, the stripped-down version answered: "your reading list was empty until just now." The accumulation did not exist.

11. Finally, we ran a moving experiment. A real workspace, nurtured over several weeks, held forty-three apps and more than twelve hundred records. From it, we exported only what memory-portability protocols can carry — the conversation memory — and moved it into a fresh environment. Every piece of conversation memory survived. But apps: zero. Records: zero. Reminders and other automation: zero. When we asked the relocated assistant to do the same jobs as before, it completed none of the four. The most striking moment came when we asked about an upcoming payment. From its chat history, the assistant could recall that a reminder had existed — and it admitted, itself, that it could no longer deliver one. Moving the memory moves the memories, not the abilities. The relocated assistant could talk about its old life; it could not return to it.

12. Of course, raising an assistant at home takes some effort, and we want to be honest about that too. You cannot just log in; you have to install it on your own computer. Backup is your job as well — though the whole workspace is a single folder, and in practice today's users back it up with git, change history and all. The assistant also lives on one machine. And one thing should not be forgotten: the AI model — the brain — is still rented. But the workspace is built so any model can use it. MulmoClaude runs on Claude Code today, a GPT-based engine is in beta on the same workspace, and support for open models is planned. Swap the engine, and everything you have grown stays yours.

13. Where will your memories, your data, and your apps live? That important choice is being settled right now, without much thought, in the form of "just leave it all with whatever service everyone uses." MulmoClaude offers a different choice — one that actually works. The AI builds the apps. A host on your own computer runs them. And everything that piles up stays as your own files. An assistant that knows everything about you and supports you around the clock is still not sold anywhere. But there is now a way to raise one — tested in practice, and open source. The one who raises it is you.
